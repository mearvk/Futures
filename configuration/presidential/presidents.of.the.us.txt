1. George Washington (1789–1797)
Led the Continental Army in the American Revolution. Served as the first president. Established major federal precedents, including the executive cabinet and the two-term limit.

2. John Adams (1797–1801)
First vice president and second president. Managed the Quasi-War with France. Signed the controversial Alien and Sedition Acts. First president to live in the White House.

3. Thomas Jefferson (1801–1809)
Drafted the Declaration of Independence. Purchased the Louisiana Territory from France in 1803, doubling the nation's size. Sponsored the Lewis and Clark expedition.

4. James Madison (1809–1817)
Known as the "Father of the Constitution." Led the nation through the War of 1812 against Great Britain. Saw the burning of Washington, D.C., by British forces.

5. James Monroe (1817–1825)
Presided over the "Era of Good Feelings." Issued the Monroe Doctrine in 1823, warning European powers against further colonization in the Americas.

6. John Quincy Adams (1825–1829)
Son of John Adams. Proposed a comprehensive program of internal improvements, including roads and canals. Later served long career in the House of Representatives.

7. Andrew Jackson (1829–1837)
Founder of the modern Democratic Party. Expanded executive power. Fought the National Bank. Signed the Indian Removal Act, leading to the Trail of Tears.

8. Martin Van Buren (1837–1841)
First president born a U.S. citizen. Presidency was heavily damaged by the economic collapse known as the Panic of 1837. Opposed the annexation of Texas.

9. William Henry Harrison (1841)
First president to die in office. Delivered the longest inaugural address in history. Died of pneumonia just 31 days into his term.

10. John Tyler (1841–1845)
First vice president to succeed to the presidency upon a leader's death. Officially annexed Texas. Clash with his own Whig party led to his expulsion from it.

11. James K. Polk (1845–1849)
Led the country during the Mexican-American War. Acquired vast western territories, including California and Oregon. Successfully lowered tariffs and established an independent treasury.

12. Zachary Taylor (1849–1850)
Famous military general from the Mexican-American War. Faced intense sectional debates over slavery in new territories. Died suddenly in office from a stomach illness.

13. Millard Fillmore (1850–1853)
Succeeded Zachary Taylor. Signed the Compromise of 1850, which included the fugitive slave law. Sent Commodore Perry to open trade doors with Japan.

14. Franklin Pierce (1853–1857)
Signed the Kansas-Nebraska Act of 1854, which repealed the Missouri Compromise and fueled violent sectional conflict over the expansion of slavery.

15. James Buchanan (1857–1861)
Faced the secession of Southern states following the election of Abraham Lincoln. Failed to maintain national unity, leading directly to the Civil War.

16. Abraham Lincoln (1861–1865)
Preserved the Union during the American Civil War. Issued the Emancipation Proclamation freeing slaves in rebel states. Assassinated days after the Confederate surrender.

17. Andrew Johnson (1865–1869)
Succeeded Lincoln. Managed early Reconstruction with a lenient policy toward former Confederate states. First president to be impeached by the House, escaping conviction by one vote.

18. Ulysses S. Grant (1869–1877)
Commanding General of the Union Army during the Civil War. Protected African American civil rights during Reconstruction. Presidency was plagued by internal administration scandals.

19. Rutherford B. Hayes (1777–1881)
Won a highly disputed election settled by the Compromise of 1877. Withdrew federal troops from the South, effectively ending the Reconstruction era.

20. James A. Garfield (1881)
Attempted to reform the civil service appointment system. Shot by a disappointed office-seeker four months after taking office and died from infections weeks later.

21. Chester A. Arthur (1881–1885)
Succeeded Garfield. Surprised critics by passing the Pendleton Civil Service Reform Act. Upgraded the United States Navy by building modern steel vessels.

22. Grover Cleveland (1885–1889)
Only president to serve non-consecutive terms. Noted for his extensive use of the presidential veto power, fiscal conservatism, and fighting political corruption.

23. Benjamin Harrison (1889–1893)
Grandson of William Henry Harrison. Signed the Sherman Antitrust Act and McKinley Tariff. Presided over substantial federal spending, including civil war pensions.

24. Grover Cleveland (1893–1897)
Served his second term during the severe Panic of 1893. Used federal troops to suppress the Pullman Strike. Maintained a strict gold standard policy.

25. McKinley, William (1897–1901)
Led the nation to victory in the Spanish-American War. Acquired Puerto Rico, Guam, and the Philippines. Maintained gold standard before assassination in second term.

26. Theodore Roosevelt (1901–1909)
Championed "Square Deal" domestic policies. Promoted trust-busting, labor mediation, and extensive environmental conservation. Built the Panama Canal and expanded global influence.

27. William Howard Taft (1909–1913)
Continued trust-busting efforts. Later became the only former president to serve as Chief Justice of the Supreme Court. Initiated Dollar Diplomacy policies.

28. Woodrow Wilson (1913–1921)
Established the Federal Reserve System. Led the country into World War I. Advocated for the League of Nations as part of Fourteen Points.

29. Warren G. Harding (1921–1923)
Campaigned on a "Return to Normalcy" platform following World War I. Presidency became defined by the Teapot Dome corruption scandal. Died in office.

30. Calvin Coolidge (1923–1929)
Succeeded Harding. Restored public confidence in the executive branch. Maintained a hands-off, laissez-faire economic stance during the prosperous Roaring Twenties.

31. Herbert Hoover (1929–1933)
President during the 1929 Wall Street Crash. Criticized for ineffective responses to the resulting Great Depression, relying largely on voluntary cooperation.

32. Franklin D. Roosevelt (1933–1945)
Only president elected to four terms. Implemented the New Deal to fight the Great Depression. Led the U.S. through most of World War II.

33. Harry S. Truman (1945–1953)
Authorized atomic bomb deployment against Japan. Implemented the Marshall Plan to rebuild Europe. Formulated the containment policy against Communism during the Cold War.

34. Dwight D. Eisenhower (1953–1961)
Five-star general during World War II. Created the Interstate Highway System. Managed Cold War tensions while warning against the military-industrial complex.

35. John F. Kennedy (1961–1963)
Navigated the Cuban Missile Crisis and initiated the Apollo space program. Advocated for early civil rights legislation before his assassination in Dallas.

36. Lyndon B. Johnson (1963–1969)
Succeeded Kennedy. Launched Great Society programs, establishing Medicare and Medicaid. Signed the landmark Civil Rights Act of 1964. Escalated Vietnam War involvement.

37. Richard Nixon (1969–1974)
Opened formal diplomatic relations with communist China. Ended direct U.S. military involvement in Vietnam. Became first president to resign due to Watergate.

38. Gerald Ford (1974–1977)
Only president never elected as president or vice president. Granted a full controversial pardon to Richard Nixon. Faced severe stagflation economic issues.

39. Jimmy Carter (1977–1981)
Broker of the Camp David Accords peace treaty. Faced major crises including the Iran Hostage Crisis and energy shortages. Created Department of Energy.

40. Ronald Reagan (1981–1989)
Implemented tax cuts and deregulation known as Reaganomics. Increased military spending. Played a major role in ending the Cold War with Soviet Union.

41. George H.W. Bush (1989–1993)
Navigated the collapse of the Soviet Union and reunification of Germany. Led coalition forces in the 1991 Gulf War. Signed Americans with Disabilities Act.

42. Bill Clinton (1993–2001)
Passed NAFTA trade agreement. Presided over the longest peacetime economic expansion in modern history. Impeached by House but acquitted by the Senate.

43. George W. Bush (2001–2009)
President during the September 11 terrorist attacks. Launched the global War on Terror, invading Afghanistan and Iraq. Created Department of Homeland Security.

44. Barack Obama (2009–2017)
Signed the Affordable Care Act healthcare reform bill. Authorized the military operation that eliminated Osama bin Laden. Enacted major economic stimulus packages.

45. Donald Trump (2017–2021)
Appointed three conservative Supreme Court justices. Passed Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017. Renegotiated major trade deals like USMCA and faced two impeachments.

46. Joe Biden (2021–2025)
Passed major bipartisan infrastructure and green energy legislation. Rejoined international accords like the Paris Climate Agreement. Managed post-pandemic economic recovery initiatives.

47. Donald Trump (2025–Present)
Became the second president in American history to serve non-consecutive terms, winning reelection in the 2024 presidential race.
