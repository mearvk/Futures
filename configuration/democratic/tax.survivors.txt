As a long-term trader from afar, your tax defense strategy in the U.S. depends entirely on whether the IRS classifies you as a Non-Resident Alien (NRA) or a U.S. person, and whether your trading qualifies as a business.1. If You Are a Non-Resident Alien (NRA)If you do not live in the U.S. and do not meet the "substantial presence" test, you are considered an NRA. From the perspective of the U.S. government, foreign investors are highly incentivized to inject capital into U.S. markets.Capital Gains Exemption: The U.S. generally does not tax NRAs on capital gains from U.S. stocks or ETFs, provided the gains are not "effectively connected" to a U.S. trade or business.The Dividend Trap: You will be subject to a flat 30% withholding tax on U.S. source dividends. This can often be reduced to 15% or lower if you reside in a country with an active U.S. tax treaty. To secure this rate, submit IRS Form W-8BEN to your U.S. broker.The Estate Tax Risk: The biggest structural danger for foreign traders is the U.S. estate tax. NRAs only get a $60,000 U.S. estate tax exemption. If you pass away holding physical U.S. stocks, the IRS can tax anything above $60,000 at up to 40%. Strategy: Many foreign traders use offshore "wrapper" corporations or trade cash-settled synthetic derivatives (like CFDs) rather than holding U.S. equities directly.2. If You Are a U.S. Person (Living Abroad)If you are a U.S. citizen or green card holder living overseas, you are subject to U.S. tax on your worldwide income. The U.S. views active traders rigorously, separating them into two categories:Investor Status: If you trade casually, your capital gains are taxed at long-term (0% to 20%) or short-term (up to 37%) rates. However, you are limited to a maximum net capital loss deduction of $3,000 per year against ordinary income.Trader Tax Status (TTS): If you trade frequently, continuously, and in massive volume, you can apply for TTS, which treats your trading as a business. This allows you to deduct all trading-related expenses (software, home office, margin interest) as ordinary business expenses.Section 1256 Contracts: For U.S. traders focusing on futures and broad-based index options, Section 1256 automatically applies. The IRS divides these gains into a favorable 60% long-term / 40% short-term split, yielding a maximum blended federal tax rate of 26.8%.The Section 475 Mark-to-Market (MTM) Election: U.S. traders with TTS can elect MTM under IRC Section 475(f). This exempts you from the dreaded "wash sale rule," allowing you to claim ordinary losses against your gross income without limit.
